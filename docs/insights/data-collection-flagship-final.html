What is GBIF whale data? Accuracy, limits, and why it matters

Home/Article
The Data

How whale sightings data
is collected globally

Whale data is not created in one place. It is built from many systems working independently across the world — research vessels, satellites, acoustic sensors, public reports, and global biodiversity platforms. This guide explains how each layer works and how they combine to form what we call “whale data.”

Quick context

Whale data is a system, not a dataset.

No single method captures whales globally. Each approach solves a different problem — visibility, movement, presence, or scale. The real value comes from combining them carefully.

The short answer

Whale sightings data is collected through a combination of scientific surveys, satellite tagging, acoustic monitoring, citizen science observations, and global aggregation systems such as GBIF and OBIS. Each method captures a different dimension of whale presence.

No single system provides a complete view. What we call whale data is the result of combining multiple incomplete perspectives into something usable.

Why there is no single whale dataset

Unlike something like GPS tracking of vehicles, whales move through vast, difficult-to-observe environments. There is no global sensor network tracking every whale.

This means whale data must be assembled from independent efforts across time and geography. Each effort answers a specific question, not the entire system.

  • Where are whales seen?
  • How do they move?
  • Are they present acoustically?
  • How has distribution changed over time?

Scientific surveys (the foundation)

Marine surveys are the backbone of whale data. Researchers observe whales directly using ships or aircraft.

These surveys are structured and repeatable, which makes them highly reliable. However, they are limited by cost, time, and geography.

They provide high-quality snapshots — not continuous global coverage.

Satellite tagging (movement layer)

Satellite tagging allows scientists to track individual whales over time.

This provides extremely valuable movement data, showing migration routes and behavior patterns.

However, only a small number of whales are tagged, meaning this data is deep but narrow.

Acoustic monitoring (presence layer)

Hydrophones detect whale sounds underwater, allowing researchers to identify whale presence without visual observation.

This expands coverage significantly but introduces interpretation challenges, such as distinguishing species and estimating location.

Citizen science (scale layer)

Public sightings dramatically increase geographic coverage.

While individual records vary in quality, they provide valuable real-world signals when aggregated.

They are especially important in regions with limited scientific survey coverage.

Global aggregation systems (access layer)

Platforms like GBIF and OBIS bring together records from multiple sources.

This makes whale data accessible at scale, but also introduces inconsistencies and requires interpretation.

These systems are discovery layers, not perfect datasets.

Why combining sources matters

Each method captures a different truth:

  • Surveys = accuracy
  • Tagging = movement
  • Acoustics = presence
  • Citizen science = coverage
  • Aggregation = accessibility

Combining them creates a more complete understanding.

Limitations of whale data collection

Every method has limitations:

  • Geographic gaps
  • Species bias
  • Temporal gaps
  • Measurement uncertainty

Understanding these limitations is essential for correct interpretation.
