OBIS vs GBIF:
which whale dataset should you use?

OBIS and GBIF are the two most commonly used gateways for whale-related records. They overlap, but they are not the same. This guide explains how each system works, where each one shines, where each one fails, and how to use both together without misleading yourself or your users.

The short answer

GBIF is a global biodiversity discovery platform. OBIS is a marine biodiversity system. For whales, GBIF offers reach and accessibility, while OBIS offers ocean-focused context. Neither is universally better — they are useful in different ways.

If you need a rule: start with GBIF for discovery, use OBIS to strengthen marine interpretation, and combine both carefully.

What GBIF is (and is not)

GBIF aggregates occurrence records across all species. It exposes records from museums, research programs, surveys, and citizen science. It is best understood as a discovery layer, not a single curated whale database.

Strengths include scale, accessibility, and breadth. Limitations include uneven data quality, mixed precision, and the need for filtering before public visualization.

What OBIS is (and is not)

OBIS focuses on marine biodiversity. It integrates datasets aligned with ocean science workflows and often carries metadata that is more directly relevant to marine contexts.

Strengths include marine-first structure and alignment with oceanographic research. Limitations include coverage gaps and dependence on contributing datasets.

How their data pipelines differ

Both systems aggregate from many sources, but their pipelines differ in emphasis and curation.

  • GBIF: broad ingestion across biodiversity publishers, strong standardization for discoverability.
  • OBIS: marine-oriented ingestion, often closer to oceanographic datasets and standards.

These differences affect how records appear, how complete they are for marine use, and how much post-processing is required.

Coverage and gaps

GBIF typically has wider global reach across many taxa and contributors. OBIS may have stronger coverage in marine research corridors but can be patchy elsewhere.

For whales, this means you may find:

  • GBIF: more records overall, more heterogeneity
  • OBIS: fewer but more marine-aligned records in some regions

Accuracy and data quality

Neither system guarantees perfect accuracy. Quality depends on source datasets and how records were collected and processed.

Common issues include coordinate errors, georeferencing uncertainty, duplicate records, and inconsistent metadata.

Overlap and duplication

Because both systems ingest from overlapping communities, the same underlying observation can appear in both — sometimes with slight differences.

Without deduplication, combining OBIS and GBIF can inflate counts or create visual clutter.

Good workflows include:

  • deduplicating by identifiers or spatiotemporal proximity
  • prioritizing higher-precision records
  • tracking source provenance

When each is the better choice

Use GBIF when:

  • you need a broad starting point
  • you are building discovery tools
  • you want maximum coverage

Use OBIS when:

  • you need marine-focused context
  • you are analyzing ocean patterns
  • you want alignment with marine datasets

Why using both is usually best

The strongest systems do not choose one. They combine both, then apply filtering and explanation.

GBIF provides reach. OBIS provides marine framing. Together, they produce a more useful picture — if handled carefully.

For WhaleData

Combine sources, remove impossible points, flag uncertainty, and explain what users are seeing. That is how maps become trustworthy.

Practical implementation tips

  • Normalize coordinate systems and precision
  • Filter known geospatial issues
  • Separate historical vs modern records
  • Expose uncertainty to users
  • Link back to source datasets

Frequently asked questions

Is OBIS better than GBIF for whales?

Not universally. OBIS is often stronger for marine context, while GBIF offers broader reach.

Should I use both?

Yes, in most cases combining both with filtering yields the best result.

Why do records differ between them?

Because they ingest and standardize data differently and may receive different versions of the same source datasets.
