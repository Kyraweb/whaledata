appear on land

This is one of the most confusing things people notice in whale maps: a whale appears in the middle of a continent. It looks absurd, but it is also one of the best entry points into understanding how biodiversity data actually works. These points are not signs that whales are on land. They are signs that data needs interpretation.

Quick context

The short answer

Whales appear on land in maps because of geospatial data issues, not because whales are actually there. In most cases, the problem comes from how a record was collected, converted into coordinates, standardized inside a dataset, or displayed on a map.

That may sound technical, but the idea is simple: a map point is only as reliable as the process that created it. If a whale record passes through uncertain location descriptions, rough coordinate estimates, historical sources, or inconsistent data pipelines, it can end up in the wrong place — sometimes very obviously wrong.

This is why a whale point on land should not immediately make you reject a dataset. It should make you ask better questions about the data behind the map.

Why people notice this so quickly

Most biodiversity data errors are subtle. A point might be slightly misplaced offshore, shifted too close to shore, or located in the wrong part of a region. Whales on land are different. They are visually dramatic. Even someone with no data background can tell that a blue whale should not be sitting in the middle of a city or deep inland valley.

That makes whale maps uniquely useful for teaching data literacy. The error is obvious enough that it forces a question: if this point is wrong, what does that say about the rest of the map?

The answer is not that the whole system is worthless. The answer is that biodiversity data has to be interpreted. A map is not reality. A map is a visualization of recorded information, and recorded information always carries the limits of how it was created.

A whale on land is not usually a biological mystery. It is a data pipeline story made visible.

How map points actually work

It helps to start with a basic idea: a point on a map is not the same thing as a living event. It is a recorded location. That location is usually represented by latitude and longitude, which are just numbers describing a place on Earth.

Those numbers only become meaningful if several things go right:

  • the location was recorded correctly in the first place,
  • the coordinates were entered in the right fields,
  • the record used the right coordinate system,
  • the numbers were not swapped, rounded, or transformed incorrectly,
  • the map software displayed them in the expected projection.

If any one of those steps fails, the point can move. Sometimes it only moves a little. Sometimes it moves enough to place a marine animal on land.

This matters because people often trust mapped data more than tabular data. A spreadsheet feels uncertain. A map feels definitive. But maps can hide uncertainty just as easily as they can reveal patterns.

Coordinate errors are the simplest explanation

Some whale points on land come from very plain mistakes. Coordinates may be entered incorrectly, copied badly, or stored in the wrong order. A latitude value entered where longitude belongs, or vice versa, can send a point to the wrong side of the planet.

There are also smaller problems that still matter:

  • missing decimal points,
  • sign errors that flip north to south or east to west,
  • rounding that removes critical precision near coastlines,
  • default coordinates assigned when a source lacked a precise location.

Marine records are especially vulnerable near coastlines because the boundary between land and water is visually unforgiving. A slight shift might not matter in open ocean, but near a coastline it can instantly become obvious.

Important: a point that looks ridiculous on a map may still come from a real underlying record. The problem may be the location handling, not the existence of the record itself.

Georeferencing is where uncertainty becomes visible

Not every whale record started life with GPS coordinates. Many records were described in words long before they were converted into modern map-ready points. That conversion process is called georeferencing.

Imagine an old record that says “off the coast of British Columbia” or “near Vancouver Island.” That description may be useful, but it is not a precise point. To visualize it, someone has to translate the description into coordinates. The moment that happens, interpretation enters the system.

And interpretation means uncertainty.

Even a good georeferencing decision can still place a record slightly inland, especially if the place description was vague, the coastline changed across map references, or the source never intended that record to be interpreted as a highly precise point.

This is one reason why old whale records, historical marine observations, and archive-based datasets should be treated differently from modern GPS-based sightings. They may be equally valuable for broad pattern recognition, but they do not carry the same location precision.

Historical whale records are incredibly useful — and often spatially messy

Historical data is one of the most exciting parts of whale research because it helps people understand how whale distribution may have changed over time. But it also creates some of the biggest mapping headaches.

Old whale observations may come from ship logs, field notes, handwritten surveys, museum records, or digitized archives. These sources were not built for modern web mapping. They were built for the needs of their own time.

That means:

  • place descriptions may be approximate,
  • location methods may be inconsistent,
  • precision may be impossible to recover fully,
  • later digitization may introduce new uncertainty.

When these records are plotted beside modern GPS-quality data, the contrast becomes obvious. A historical record may still be scientifically meaningful, but it should not always be treated like a precise modern sighting pin.

This is why strong whale platforms should separate “record exists” from “record is precisely mappable”.

Aggregation across datasets makes the problem easier to see

Large platforms like GBIF and more marine-focused systems like OBIS are useful partly because they bring together many sources. That is also why they can surface location problems. Once many records from many institutions are displayed together, inconsistencies become visible.

Each source may have its own:

  • metadata standards,
  • taxonomic conventions,
  • coordinate precision,
  • quality control practices,
  • assumptions about intended use.

Aggregation does not create all those problems from nothing. It exposes them. What looked acceptable inside one source system may become obviously odd once placed beside records from other systems on a shared global map.

This is why it is a mistake to ask whether a single odd point proves an aggregated dataset is bad. A better question is: what kinds of records should be filtered, flagged, or interpreted differently once many sources are combined?

Projection and visualization can add another layer of confusion

The Earth is curved. Maps are flat. To display the Earth, software has to use a projection, which always involves some kind of distortion. Most of the time that distortion is manageable, but projection handling still matters when coordinates are already uncertain.

If a dataset was prepared under one spatial assumption and then displayed in another system incorrectly, points can shift. Coastlines can also exaggerate the visual effect of a small error. A location that is only slightly off in open water may suddenly appear unacceptable when it crosses a shoreline boundary.

For users, the lesson is not that projections make maps unusable. The lesson is that map display is never the whole story. A point is the end result of collection, conversion, aggregation, and visualization — not just one of those things.

This is not unique to whales — whales just make it obvious

Birds, fish, plants, insects, and terrestrial mammals can all carry location issues in large biodiversity systems. But whale data creates especially memorable examples because the mismatch between animal and location is so dramatic.

A whale on land feels absurd. That makes it a good teaching case. It reminds people that data quality is not an abstract concern. It changes what users believe when they look at a map.

In that sense, these errors are valuable. They make invisible data assumptions visible.

Why filtering matters more than people realize

A raw dataset is not a finished whale map. It is input material. The real quality of a public-facing system often depends less on the existence of the data and more on what happens next.

A responsible whale platform should ask questions like:

  • Should impossible terrestrial points be removed?
  • Should records with known geospatial issues be excluded by default?
  • Should historical and modern records be displayed differently?
  • Should uncertainty be visible to the user?
  • Should certain species be cross-checked against additional marine-focused sources?

These are not cosmetic decisions. They shape whether a map educates or misleads.

Why this matters for WhaleData

WhaleData should not try to hide the complexity of real whale records. It should do something better: use filtering and explanation to make the complexity understandable. That is how a platform becomes trustworthy rather than merely visual.

Does this mean whale data cannot be trusted?

No. It means whale data should be used intelligently.

There is a big difference between saying “some points are wrong” and saying “the entire dataset is useless.” Large biodiversity systems can still be incredibly valuable for pattern recognition, education, species distribution questions, and research workflows — even if some records need caution.

Trust should come from understanding:

  • where the data came from,
  • what level of precision it carries,
  • what filtering has been applied,
  • what the platform is honest about.

In fact, a platform that openly explains why whales appear on land may deserve more trust, not less, because it is showing users how the system really works.

The best way to think about whale points on land

The wrong way to think about these points is: “The data is fake.”

The better way to think about them is: “This record needs context.”

That shift is powerful because it changes the user from a passive viewer into an informed interpreter. Instead of asking whether the map is simply right or wrong, you begin asking:

  • What was the original source?
  • How was the location determined?
  • How precise was it meant to be?
  • What happened to it on the way into the final visualization?

Those are the questions that lead to better whale maps, better educational content, and better public understanding.

Frequently asked questions

Why do whale sightings appear on land in maps?

Because of geospatial data issues such as coordinate mistakes, georeferencing uncertainty, historical records, and differences in how datasets are aggregated and visualized.

Does this mean the whale data is fake?

No. It usually means the data needs interpretation. A record can still be useful even if its mapped point is visually misleading or spatially imprecise.

Can GBIF or OBIS contain whale points on land?

Yes. Large biodiversity datasets can contain geospatial issues, especially when they combine records from many sources with different standards and levels of precision.

How should whale maps handle these errors?

A responsible whale map should filter impossible coordinates, flag uncertain records, explain data limitations, and avoid presenting every point as equally trustworthy.
