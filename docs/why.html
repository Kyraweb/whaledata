Why whales.
Why now.

The conservation context that led to whaledata.org — why cetacean data matters, why the existing tools are failing, and how a solo developer and a passion for the ocean turned into 25,000 records on a live globe.

Why whales?

Whales occupy a position in the natural world that is difficult to overstate. The blue whale — at up to 33 metres and 150 tonnes — is the largest animal that has ever existed on Earth, exceeding the size of any dinosaur on record. But size alone doesn't explain why they matter. Whales are ecological infrastructure.

A single great whale circulates nutrients across ocean layers on a scale that affects entire marine food webs. When a whale dives, it brings nutrients from the deep up to the surface — a process called the whale pump — fertilising phytoplankton that produce a significant fraction of the world's oxygen. When a whale dies and sinks to the ocean floor, the whale fall creates an entire ecosystem that sustains hundreds of species for decades.

And yet, despite their size and ecological importance, whales remain poorly understood. They spend the vast majority of their lives below the surface, in deep water, across oceanic distances that no monitoring network can fully cover. A humpback whale swimming from its winter breeding grounds near Hawaii to its summer feeding grounds in the Gulf of Alaska covers roughly 5,000 kilometres — and does so with a consistency that has barely changed in thousands of years.

"We know these routes exist. We do not always know why a particular whale deviated from one, or stopped appearing in a region it had always used."

The conservation picture for cetaceans is mixed and in some cases alarming. Blue whales — once abundant across all major oceans — were reduced to perhaps 1% of their original population by industrial whaling in the 20th century. The species remains classified as Endangered by the IUCN.

Fin whales, also heavily hunted, are Vulnerable. Grey whales undertake the longest migration of any mammal — up to 20,000 kilometres round-trip — yet the Western Pacific population is critically endangered with fewer than 100 individuals remaining. Orca populations in the Pacific Northwest are declining due to prey depletion, vessel noise, and chemical contaminants.

The data problem

One of the most significant obstacles to effective whale conservation is that the data is scattered. Researchers at one institution maintain records of thousands of sightings that are never systematically shared with researchers at another. Government agencies collect stranding data that sits in national databases largely inaccessible to international researchers. Acoustic monitoring networks generate continuous detection records that are difficult to correlate with visual sighting data.

Historical whaling records — some of the richest longitudinal data available about whale distribution — exist in archives and digitised logbooks that have never been plotted on a single map alongside modern observations.

The gap is not data. It's interface.

Organisations like GBIF and OBIS have made enormous progress in aggregating biodiversity data. GBIF holds over 2 billion occurrence records. OBIS holds over 100 million marine species records. iNaturalist has accumulated over 200 million observations. The data exists. The tools to explore it accessibly — without writing SQL queries or downloading CSV files — largely do not.

A conservation advocacy organisation trying to illustrate why a particular shipping lane needs rerouting cannot easily generate a map showing the overlap of vessel traffic, whale feeding grounds, and historical stranding events in that corridor. The data to make that argument exists. The interface to present it does not. That is the gap whaledata.org is attempting to fill.

The origin of this project

I am not a marine biologist. I am a web developer and designer who runs a small agency — KyraWeb — building websites and digital tools for organisations. I have a long-standing personal interest in marine conservation, and I have watched with frustration as the tools available for public engagement with ocean data lagged behind what the underlying datasets could support.

The specific motivation for whaledata.org was a conversation with a conservation researcher trying to visualise whale sighting data for a public presentation. They had the data. They could not make it look like anything. They ended up with a static image from Google My Maps that showed a few dozen points and nothing else. The datasets that existed — GBIF, OBIS, historical whaling logs — could have produced something genuinely compelling. The barrier was not data access. It was the gap between raw data and meaningful presentation.

"That conversation planted a seed. About twelve months later, whaledata.org exists."

The project was built over approximately twelve months — from the initial schema design in late 2024 through to a full production platform with automated data pipelines, a conservation layer system, email alerts, and this documentation site. Every decision, every debugging session, and every data quality problem is documented across the remaining pages of this site.

The build was completed with assistance from Claude by Anthropic — used for architecture decisions, code generation, debugging, and documentation throughout. This is an example of what becomes possible when domain knowledge and AI-assisted development work together toward a clear goal.

What whaledata.org actually does

In practical terms, whaledata.org provides:

A unified view of 25,000+ records from five open scientific databases — GBIF, OBIS, NOAA, iNaturalist, and historical whaling archives — rendered on a real-time 3D globe with species-colour-coded dots, animated migration routes, and clustering that responds to zoom level.

Conservation context layers that can be toggled alongside the sighting data — feeding ground polygons, naval sonar exercise zones, and 32 major shipping corridors — making the overlaps between human activity and whale habitat immediately visible without any additional analysis.

A public API at api.whaledata.org that exposes all the data programmatically, enabling researchers and developers to build their own tools on top of the same dataset.

A weekly email alert system that notifies subscribers when new sighting records are added matching their species, region, or data layer preferences — turning a passive visualisation into an active monitoring tool.
