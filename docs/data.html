GBIF

Global Biodiversity Information Facility

gbif.org — established 2001 — Copenhagen, Denmark
5,590 records CC BY 4.0 Updated weekly Primary sightings source

GBIF was established in 2001 through an OECD initiative to make global biodiversity data freely available. It aggregates data from over 2,000 publishing institutions globally, covering more than 2 billion individual occurrence records across all taxa. For whaledata.org, GBIF is the primary source for modern sighting records across all six species.

The GBIF API allows queries by species taxon key, date range, geographic bounding box, and quality flags. Each record contains a latitude, longitude, date, and source institution, along with quality metadata indicating whether the record has been flagged for geospatial issues.

Quality filtering

All queries use hasGeospatialIssue=false to exclude records with known coordinate problems — land-based coordinates, country centroids, and ambiguous georeferencing. Without this flag, whales appear in the Himalayas.

Taxon keys

GBIF uses internal taxon keys: Humpback 2440898, Blue 2440718, Orca 2440483, Grey 2440714, Sperm 2440764, Fin 2440706. The Orca key was incorrect in early versions — 2440522 — returning wrong species data.

⚠️

Known limitation: Even with geospatial filtering active, a small cluster of records appeared in landlocked South America (Paraguay/Bolivia). These were caught by a secondary coordinate filter applied at the frontend. Colombia and Ecuador Pacific coast records — which appear inland due to globe projection angle — are genuine coastal sightings and are preserved.

OBIS

Ocean Biodiversity Information System

obis.org — UNESCO IOC — 100M+ marine records
2,000 records CC BY 4.0 Updated weekly Fin whale supplement

OBIS emerged from the Census of Marine Life and now operates under the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission of UNESCO. It holds over 100 million occurrence records specifically for marine species — complementary to GBIF rather than competing.

For whaledata.org, OBIS serves as the primary source for Fin whale data, supplementing GBIF which has thinner coverage for this species. OBIS also acts as the aggregation layer for NOAA stranding data.

AphiaIDs (WoRMS)

OBIS uses World Register of Marine Species identifiers. Fin whale AphiaID: 137092. Humpback: 137091. These are different from GBIF taxon keys and cannot be used interchangeably.

Date parsing

OBIS date formats are inconsistent across datasets — full ISO date, year-month, year-only, and date ranges all appear. The sync script handles all formats with sequential fallback, storing NULL rather than crashing on unknown formats.

NOAA

National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration

Strandings via OBIS · Acoustics via NOAA PACM
~12,000 strandings ~750 acoustic detections Public Domain Monthly sync

NOAA contributes two distinct data types to whaledata.org: stranding events from the National Stranding Database, and acoustic detections from the Passive Acoustic Cetacean Monitoring (PACM) programme.

Strandings: The NOAA stranding network has recorded every reported cetacean stranding in US waters since 1972. Records include condition (alive/dead/unknown), species, location, and date. The dataset flows into OBIS which makes it accessible via their aggregation API. It currently provides approximately 12,000 records across the six tracked species.

Acoustics: PACM deploys hydrophone networks across US waters, listening continuously for whale vocalisations — humpback songs, sperm whale clicks, blue whale contact calls. Acoustic detection can occur in complete darkness, at any depth, and at ranges of hundreds of kilometres, making it essential for deep-diving species that spend little time at the surface.

ℹ️

PACM access note: The NOAA PACM API was unreachable from the hosting network at deployment time. Acoustic data is therefore sourced through OBIS, which aggregates PACM records. This provides ~750 records currently rather than the several thousand that direct PACM access would supply. This limitation is being monitored for resolution.

iNat

iNaturalist

inaturalist.org — California Academy of Sciences + National Geographic
3,300 records CC BY-NC 4.0 Updated weekly Research-grade only

iNaturalist is a citizen science platform where users submit photo observations that are verified by a community of expert identifiers. Records confirmed to species level by multiple community members receive a research-grade designation — the only quality tier included in whaledata.org.

The research-grade filter is essential. iNaturalist has tens of millions of records, many of which are speculative identifications or insufficiently documented for species confirmation. The research-grade subset represents observations where there is genuine community consensus.

Grey whale has the highest iNaturalist count (~1,200) because Grey whale populations are primarily coastal in the Pacific — accessible to observers from boats and shorelines. Orca follows (~797) for similar reasons. Blue whale, a deep-ocean species, has relatively few iNaturalist records despite being critically important for conservation.

Taxon IDs

iNaturalist uses its own internal identifiers: Humpback 41472, Blue 41467, Fin 41468, Grey 41476, Sperm 41490, Orca 41446. Early versions had incorrect IDs for Blue whale and Orca — the resulting records showed completely wrong geographic distributions before the errors were caught and the data wiped and re-synced.

Citizen science value

Research-grade iNaturalist records often capture sightings in coastal areas that are under-represented in formal survey programmes. Recreational boaters, whale watching operators, and coastal residents contribute observations that fill geographic gaps in the scientific literature.

1785

Historical Records — Whaling Logs

GBIF pre-1950 datasets — digitised 19th and early 20th century records
~1,500 records CC BY 4.0 Updated yearly Distribution baseline

The historical layer is the most scientifically compelling dataset in the platform. Pre-1950 records from digitised 19th and early 20th century whaling logs provide a distribution picture that predates industrial hunting by over a century — something no modern dataset alone can offer.

These records originate from multiple sources: digitised logs from New England whaling vessels, records from early scientific surveys, sightings recorded in naval logs, and naturalist observations. They are heavily biased toward the North Atlantic, South Atlantic, and Pacific whaling grounds that were accessible to sailing ships — but they tell a story that modern data cannot.

Comparing the historical distribution to modern sighting data is one of the most revealing features of the platform. Blue whale historical records cluster heavily in the Southern Ocean at high latitudes where the species was once hunted in enormous numbers. Modern sighting records are absent from most of those areas — not because blue whales have disappeared entirely, but because systematic monitoring in remote sub-Antarctic waters is extremely limited today.

GBIF year format

Historical records are queried using GBIF's year range parameter formatted as 1500,1950 — not ,1950 which caused a 422 error in early versions. The comma-separated range is required even when the lower bound is distant.

What it shows

The year range slider on the globe can be set to 1785–1950 to view only historical records. Set alongside the modern sighting layer, the geographic shift in documented distribution becomes visible — a comparison that no flat-map visualisation has presented at this scale before.

Data quality and limitations

Every dataset used by whaledata.org comes with significant caveats. Intellectual honesty about these limitations is important — especially for a platform intended to support conservation advocacy.

Observation bias is the most pervasive problem. Whales are more likely to be recorded in places where people are looking for them. The coast of California, the waters around Hawaii, and the North Atlantic near major research centres are heavily surveyed. The South Indian Ocean, the central Pacific, and the waters around Antarctica are not. Areas with sparse data may not be areas with few whales — they may simply be areas with few observers.

Temporal bias compounds this. Most records are from the last 30 years, reflecting the growth of citizen science platforms and systematic survey programmes. The 19th century records are selective survivors — logs that were preserved, translated, and digitised.

Species identification is also variable. Research-grade iNaturalist records require photo documentation and expert confirmation. But some records in all datasets rely on observer identification of animals that may have been partially visible or seen at distance. Blue whale and Fin whale, which are similar in appearance and often seen only from a distance, are more frequently misidentified than Humpback whale with its distinctive fluke pattern.

📊

When reading the globe: Absence of records in a region does not mean absence of whales. Density of records reflects survey effort as much as actual whale density. The platform presents the data honestly — it does not interpolate, predict, or extrapolate beyond what the source databases actually contain.
