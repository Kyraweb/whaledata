Overview

How whale conservation mapping works

The conservation layers in whaledata.org combine environmental data with human activity to show where whales are most at risk. Feeding grounds highlight where whales depend on seasonal prey, while shipping lanes and sonar zones reveal where human systems intersect with those habitats.

These layers are designed to be explored together — making it possible to see where collisions, acoustic disruption, or habitat pressure may occur without requiring specialist GIS tools. Explore how this connects to the data sources and the technology stack.

Feeding Grounds

Whale feeding grounds are areas where oceanographic conditions — upwelling currents, nutrient concentrations, temperature gradients — produce dense concentrations of the prey species that whales depend on. For baleen whales (Humpback, Blue, Grey, Fin), prey means krill and small schooling fish. For toothed whales (Sperm whale, Orca), prey means squid, fish, and other marine mammals.

The feeding ground polygons in whaledata.org are rendered as dashed green outlines — deliberately transparent so as not to obscure the sighting data beneath. When overlaid with the shipping lane or sonar layer, the spatial conflicts become immediately visible without requiring any additional analysis.

"A whale that cannot feed adequately during summer cannot survive the winter migration. Feeding ground protection is species-level protection."

The feeding ground polygons are approximate boundaries drawn from published research and do not reflect the full complexity of whale habitat use, which varies seasonally, interannually, and in response to oceanographic conditions. A future version of the platform will incorporate dynamic habitat predictions based on sea surface temperature and prey distribution data.

Naval Sonar Exercise Zones

The relationship between military sonar and cetacean strandings has been one of the most contentious issues in marine conservation for the past three decades. The evidence linking high-intensity active sonar exercises to mass cetacean strandings is now substantial enough to have resulted in regulatory restrictions in several jurisdictions.

The leading mechanism is acoustic trauma. Mid-frequency active sonar (MFAS) operates at intensities that can exceed 235 decibels at the source. Beaked whales appear particularly vulnerable — their anatomy makes them susceptible to gas bubble disease (analogous to decompression sickness) when they ascend rapidly from depth in response to sonar exposure.

⚠️

Documented events: The Canary Islands have experienced multiple mass beaked whale strandings correlated with NATO naval exercises. The Bahamas strandings of 2000 — which killed 17 cetaceans — were attributed by NOAA to Navy sonar exercises, prompting the first significant US regulatory response.

Reading the correlation: When the stranding layer is activated alongside the sonar zones, spatial correlation is visible in several areas. This visualisation invites investigation — it does not resolve it. Some strandings in sonar areas may have other causes. Conversely, some sonar-caused mortality never appears in stranding records at all — animals that die at depth never wash ashore.

Shipping Lanes

Ship strikes are a leading cause of death for large whales globally. The problem is structural: whales are often at or near the surface to breathe, and large commercial vessels cannot stop quickly or manoeuvre around objects at short notice. A container ship travelling at 20 knots requires several kilometres to stop — far more than any surface interval a whale would occupy.

The 32 shipping corridors in whaledata.org are rendered in orange with a glow effect at three traffic intensities. They represent the major commercial routes connecting every ocean basin — the Suez Canal corridor, the Strait of Malacca, the North Pacific great circle routes, trans-Atlantic lanes, the Panama Canal connections, and more.

"The Gulf of St. Lawrence, where northbound whale migration and heavy commercial shipping converge every spring, is one of the most acutely documented collision zones in the world."

When the shipping layer is active alongside sighting data, the overlap tells a story that is difficult to communicate in text alone. Blue whale sightings in the California Current sit directly beneath the Asia-to-US West Coast lanes. Humpback sightings in Hawaiian waters are adjacent to trans-Pacific routes. The aggregated sperm whale records in the North Atlantic overlap with the busy corridor between Europe and North America.

🗺️

Technical note: Pacific routes that cross the 180° antimeridian are split into two segments in the GeoJSON. Without this split, MapLibre GL draws the line the short way around — cutting through the poles and creating a visual spike across Alaska. The split was one of the more obscure bugs in the ship lane implementation.

Reading the overlaps

The real value of the conservation layers is not in any single layer — it is in the combinations. The platform is designed so that any layer can be activated alongside any other, allowing users to explore spatial relationships without needing GIS software or programming knowledge.

Feeding grounds + shipping lanes shows where commercial traffic crosses critical foraging habitat. The California Current — one of the most productive marine ecosystems in the world and the primary summer feeding ground for Blue whales — is also one of the busiest shipping corridors in the Pacific.

Sonar zones + strandings shows whether documented stranding clusters coincide spatially with known exercise areas. The correlation is visible in the Canary Islands, the Bahamas, and the US East Coast — though causation requires further analysis beyond what a visualisation can establish.

Historical records + feeding grounds is perhaps the most sobering combination. Overlaying 19th century Blue whale distribution from whaling logs against their current documented feeding grounds reveals how dramatically the species' range has contracted — or, more accurately, how dramatically our ability to observe them in remote areas has declined.

📌

Important caveat: Spatial correlation visible on the globe is not proof of causation. The platform presents data — it does not draw conclusions. Conservation decisions require scientific analysis beyond what any single visualisation can provide. The overlaps are starting points for investigation, not endpoints.
